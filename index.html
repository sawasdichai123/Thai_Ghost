Part Two — Food Culture

Ordering Food
in Thailand

การสั่งอาหารในประเทศไทย

Thai street vendors and restaurants expect you to customise your order — knowing the right words turns a meal into a real cultural experience.

📌 Fact: Thai vendors always expect a spice request — if you say nothing, you may get the full default heat. "ไม่เผ็ด" (not spicy) is the safest starting point for beginners!

📊 % System (ร้านชานม / บอบาชา)
No sugar at all — 0%
Barely sweet — 25%
Half sweet — 50%
Very sweet — 75% / Full — 100%
📌 Fact: Traditional street stalls use the word system. Modern bubble tea chains (ชานม, บอบาชา) switched to the % system — both are widely understood in Thailand.
💬 Key Ordering Phrases
"One ___ please"  (most common opener) → ขอ ___ หนึ่งที่ครับ/ค่ะ
"Can I have it a little less spicy?" → เผ็ดน้อยได้ไหม
"No vegetables please" → ไม่เอาผัก
"Please bag it"  (takeaway) → ใส่ถุงด้วยครับ/ค่ะ
"How much is it?" → เท่าไหร่ครับ/ค่ะ
"It's delicious!"  (vendors love this 😄) → อร่อยมากครับ/ค่ะ
📌 Tip: Men end sentences with ครับ (krap) and women with ค่ะ (kha) — these are polite particles that show respect and are always appreciated.
🔥 Thai Buzzwords — คำฮิตวัยรุ่น

Trendy Thai slang you'll hear online and on the street — drop one of these and locals will instantly know you're tuned in.

ปังpang
Amazing / On point
Praise for anything stunning — an outfit, a meal, a performance.
ฟินfin
Blissful / Loving it
That pure happy feeling — great food, music, or a perfect moment.
แซ่บzaap
Deliciously spicy / Hot
Originally Isan for tasty-spicy; now also means someone looks fierce.
เทพthep
Godlike / Pro
Someone insanely skilled — "You're so thep at this game!"
ตัวแม่tua-mae
The Queen / Icon
The top-tier, trendsetting person everyone looks up to.
หมาไม่แดกmaa-mai-daek
Not very delicious
Lit. "even dogs won't eat it" — a miserable, chaotic, rock-bottom situation.
ชิลchill
Relaxed / Easygoing
From English "chill" — laid-back vibes, no stress.
สายเปย์sai-pay
The big spender
Someone who generously pays for friends or a partner.
📌 Heads up: Slang is casual — perfect with friends, but stick to polite phrases (ครับ/ค่ะ) with elders, vendors, and at work.
